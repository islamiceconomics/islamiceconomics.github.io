By the height of the Ottoman Empire, one-third of all productive land was waqf property—irrevocable charitable endowments that funded free hospitals, universities, soup kitchens, and water infrastructure. For a thousand years, institutions like Al-Azhar University and the Suleymaniye Complex operated entirely on endowment income. No government funding. No tuition fees. Just the steady revenue from dedicated land and property. Then European colonial powers systematically dismantled this infrastructure. What we often call economic modernization frequently begins with erasing the institutional memory of what came before. The waqf system represents one of history's most sophisticated approaches to sustainable social welfare—and its destruction offers lessons about what we lose when we replace indigenous economic systems without understanding their logic.
