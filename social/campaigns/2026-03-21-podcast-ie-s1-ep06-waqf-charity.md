By the Ottoman peak, an estimated one-third of all productive land was waqf property—irrevocable charitable endowments that funded free hospitals, universities, soup kitchens, and water infrastructure for centuries. The Suleymaniye Complex. Al-Azhar University. Countless institutions that operated on a principle we've largely forgotten: dedicate productive assets to serve the community in perpetuity. Then European colonial powers systematically dismantled this system. What we lost wasn't just property—it was institutional memory about wealth's purpose. Today, as we grapple with funding education, healthcare, and infrastructure, the waqf model offers lessons about sustainable endowments and community-centered economics.
