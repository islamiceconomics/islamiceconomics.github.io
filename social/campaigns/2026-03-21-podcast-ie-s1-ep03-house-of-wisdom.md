## LinkedIn

While Europe was still centuries away from banking, 9th century Baghdad had developed a sophisticated financial ecosystem that would make modern economists pause. The Abbasid capital under Caliph Harun al-Rashid featured cheques (sakk), bills of exchange (suftaja), international money transfers, and venture capital partnerships (mudaraba) - all operating without interest. The hawala system moved money across continents on trust alone. A merchant could deposit gold in Baghdad and have his partner collect the equivalent in Cairo without any physical transfer of funds. Abu Yusuf, the era's leading jurist, wrote the world's first systematic treatise on public finance, advising the Caliph on tax policies that balanced state revenue with economic justice. This wasn't primitive commerce - it was sophisticated finance guided by ethical principles. The Islamic world had created the infrastructure of global trade while maintaining its prohibition on exploitative interest.
