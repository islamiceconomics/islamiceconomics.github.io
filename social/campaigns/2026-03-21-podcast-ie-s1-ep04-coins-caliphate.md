The most trusted currency of the medieval world wasn't minted in Constantinople or Rome. In 696 CE, Caliph Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan made a decision that would reshape global commerce for centuries. He replaced all the Byzantine and Persian coins circulating in Islamic lands with purely Islamic currency—the gold dinar and silver dirham. These coins became so trusted that merchants from Europe to Asia preferred them over their own local currencies. The Islamic monetary system demonstrated principles that modern economists would later codify: Al-Ghazali articulated what we now know as Gresham's Law, while Al-Maqrizi conducted one of history's first systematic studies of inflation. This wasn't just currency reform—it was a declaration of economic sovereignty that established trust through consistent weight, purity, and Islamic principles.
