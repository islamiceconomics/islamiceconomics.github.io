Chapter 6: General Equilibrium

Formal Theory of Islamic Economics

Building the general equilibrium framework incorporating all preceding Islamic constraints: halal consumption set, israf bounds, dunya-akhirah utility, PLS financing, gharar prohibition, ihtikar constraint.

6.1 Introduction

In this chapter, we integrate all the constraints and preferences developed in previous chapters into a comprehensive general equilibrium framework. The standard competitive equilibrium model must be modified to accommodate:

  1. Halal consumption set $X^I_i \subset \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{H}|}$ (only permitted goods)
  2. Israf bounds $[0, \bar{x}_i]$ (prohibition of extravagance)
  3. Dunya-akhirah utility $u^I_i(x_i, z_i)$ (material and spiritual dimensions)
  4. PLS financing (equity-based rather than debt-based)
  5. Gharar prohibition (elimination of uncertainty in contracts)
  6. Ihtikar constraint (hoarding prevention)

This framework demonstrates that Islamic economics is not merely a set of disconnected restrictions, but rather a coherent alternative equilibrium theory.

6.2 The Islamic Exchange Economy

Definition 6.1: Islamic Exchange Economy

An Islamic exchange economy is defined as:

$$\mathcal{E}^I = \{(X^I_i, \succsim^I_i, \omega_i)_{i=1}^n\}$$

where for each agent $i \in \{1, \ldots, n\}$:

  • $X^I_i = X_i^{halal} \cap [0, \bar{x}_i] \subset \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{H}|}$ is the Islamic consumption set (halal goods subject to israf bounds)
  • $\succsim^I_i$ are Islamic preferences satisfying Axioms 1-5 (completeness, transitivity, continuity, monotonicity on $X^I_i$, and convexity)
  • $\omega_i \gg 0$ is agent $i$'s initial endowment

Definition 6.2: Islamic Walrasian Equilibrium

An allocation $x^* = (x^*_1, \ldots, x^*_n)$ and price vector $p^* \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{H}|}_+$ constitute an Islamic Walrasian equilibrium if:

(i) Individual rationality: Each consumer $i$ maximizes utility given budget and domain constraints: $$x^*_i = \arg\max_{x_i \in X^I_i \cap [0, \bar{x}_i]} u^I_i(x_i) \text{ subject to } p^* \cdot x_i \leq p^* \cdot \omega_i$$

(ii) Market clearing: Supply equals demand: $$\sum_{i=1}^n x^*_i = \sum_{i=1}^n \omega_i$$

Theorem 6.1: Existence of Islamic Walrasian Equilibrium

Theorem (Existence): Under Axioms 1-5 for each agent $i$, if $\omega_i \gg 0$ for all $i$, an Islamic Walrasian equilibrium exists.

Proof sketch: The proof uses Kakutani's fixed point theorem on the restricted domain $\prod_{i=1}^n (X^I_i \cap [0, \bar{x}_i])$. The key steps are:

  1. Define the excess demand correspondence $Z(p) = \{z : z = \sum_i (x_i(p) - \omega_i), x_i \in \arg\max_{x \in X^I_i \cap [0, \bar{x}_i]} u^I_i(x) \text{ s.t. } p \cdot x \leq p \cdot \omega_i\}$

  2. The correspondence $Z$ is upper hemicontinuous and has compact, convex values (by Axioms 1-5 and compactness of $X^I_i \cap [0, \bar{x}_i]$)

  3. Walras' law holds: $p \cdot Z(p) = 0$ for all $p$

  4. By Kakutani's fixed point theorem, there exists $p^*$ such that $0 \in Z(p^*)$, which is an equilibrium

Critical assumption: The positivity of endowments ensures that budget constraints are not degenerate.

6.4 First Welfare Theorem with Islamic Constraints

Theorem 6.2: Islamic First Welfare Theorem

Theorem (Islamic First Welfare Theorem): Every Islamic Walrasian equilibrium is Pareto efficient relative to the Islamic feasible set

$$\mathcal{F}^I = \left\{x : x_i \in X^I_i \cap [0, \bar{x}_i], \sum_i x_i = \sum_i \omega_i\right\}$$

Proof:

Suppose $(x^*, p^*)$ is an Islamic Walrasian equilibrium but $x^*$ is not Pareto efficient in $\mathcal{F}^I$. Then there exists an allocation $x' \in \mathcal{F}^I$ such that $x'_i \succsim^I_i x^*_i$ for all $i$ with at least one strict inequality.

For each agent $i$: $$x'_i \succsim^I_i x^*_i \implies p^* \cdot x'_i \geq p^* \cdot x^*_i = p^* \cdot \omega_i$$

where the last equality follows from budget balance at equilibrium.

Therefore: $$p^* \cdot \left(\sum_i x'_i\right) \geq \sum_i p^* \cdot \omega_i = p^* \cdot \left(\sum_i \omega_i\right)$$

But $x' \in \mathcal{F}^I$ means $\sum_i x'_i = \sum_i \omega_i$, so: $$p^* \cdot \left(\sum_i x'_i\right) = p^* \cdot \left(\sum_i \omega_i\right)$$

This implies $p^* \cdot x'_i = p^* \cdot \omega_i$ for all $i$ (by budget balance). But then all inequalities above must be equalities. For the agent with the strict inequality, $x'_i \succ^I_i x^*_i$ contradicts the fact that $x^*_i$ maximizes $u^I_i$ in the budget set $\{x_i \in X^I_i : p^* \cdot x_i \leq p^* \cdot \omega_i\}$. Contradiction.

Key insight: The efficiency is relative to the constrained feasible set $\mathcal{F}^I$. The equilibrium may NOT be Pareto efficient relative to the unconstrained feasible set (without israf bounds). But this is precisely the point: Islamic constraints sacrifice some material efficiency in exchange for ethical compliance and spiritual welfare.

6.5 Second Welfare Theorem with Zakat Redistribution

Theorem 6.3: Islamic Second Welfare Theorem with Zakat

Theorem (Islamic Second Welfare Theorem with Zakat): Let $x^*$ be a Pareto efficient allocation in the Islamic feasible set $\mathcal{F}^I$. If preferences are convex (Axiom 5), then there exist:

(i) A price vector $p^* \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{H}|}_+$

(ii) A zakat transfer scheme $T = (T_1, \ldots, T_n)$ with $\sum_{i=1}^n T_i = 0$

such that $x^*$ is an equilibrium with zakat transfers: each agent $i$ maximizes utility subject to $$p^* \cdot x_i \leq p^* \cdot \omega_i + T_i, \quad x_i \in X^I_i \cap [0, \bar{x}_i]$$

Proof sketch:

  1. Since $x^*$ is Pareto efficient and preferences are continuous and convex, the set of allocations giving each agent at least their utility at $x^*$ has empty interior (by the separating hyperplane theorem).

  2. There exists a supporting hyperplane to the feasible set at $x^*$, which defines the price vector $p^*$.

  3. Define the zakat transfers as $T_i = p^* \cdot \omega_i - p^* \cdot x^*_i$. These are lump-sum transfers that balance budgets: $\sum T_i = 0$.

  4. With these transfers, each agent's budget constraint is $p^* \cdot x_i \leq p^* \cdot \omega_i + T_i = p^* \cdot x^*_i$, and by construction, $x^*_i$ maximizes utility in this budget set.

Key insight: Zakat plays the role of lump-sum transfers in the classical Second Welfare Theorem. Any Pareto efficient allocation in the Islamic feasible set can be supported as a competitive equilibrium with appropriate zakat redistribution. This shows that equity and efficiency need not conflictzakat reconciles the two.

6.6 General Equilibrium with Production

Definition 6.3: Islamic Production Economy

An Islamic production economy is an extension of the exchange economy that includes firms. The economy is:

$$\mathcal{E}^{I,prod} = \{(X^I_i, \succsim^I_i, \omega_i)_{i=1}^n, (Y^I_j)_{j=1}^m\}$$

where:

  • $(X^I_i, \succsim^I_i, \omega_i)$ define consumers as before
  • $Y^I_j = \{y_j \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{H}|} : y_j \text{ is feasible under Islamic PLS financing}\}$ is firm $j$'s Islamic production set

Islamic PLS Financing vs. Interest-Based Financing

Key difference: Rather than borrowing at a fixed interest rate $r$, Islamic firms must finance investment through Profit-Loss Sharing (PLS) contracts. This fundamentally changes the equilibrium.

Proposition 6.1: In a production economy with PLS financing, the equilibrium profit rate $\pi^*$ replaces the interest rate $r^*$ as the key price. The equilibrium is characterized by:

$$\pi^* = \text{marginal rate of return to capital in equilibrium}$$

rather than

$$r^* = \text{exogenously determined interest rate (forbidden in Islamic economics)}$$

Comparison: Interest vs. Profit Rate Equilibrium

# 6.6 General Equilibrium with Production: Comparing Interest vs. PLS

# Simple 1-good, 2-period production economy
# Period 1: Agent has endowment, can invest or consume
# Period 2: Investment yields output, agent consumes

# Parameters
omega_t1 = 100  # Endowment in period 1

# Production function: output in period 2 = f(k) where k is capital invested
def production_function(k):
    """Islamic production function with diminishing returns"""
    return 40 * np.sqrt(k)  # Concave production technology

# Marginal product of capital
def mpk(k):
    """Marginal product of capital (first derivative)"""
    epsilon = 0.01
    return (production_function(k + epsilon) - production_function(k)) / epsilon

# Capital levels to consider
k_range = np.linspace(1, 100, 500)

6.7 Existence and Uniqueness of Islamic Walrasian Equilibria

Conditions for Existence

We have established existence under Axioms 1-5. The key condition is: $$\omega_i \gg 0 \text{ for all } i$$

This ensures that the budget constraint is not degenerate and agents have positive value of their endowments at any positive price vector.

Conditions for Uniqueness

Proposition 6.2: The set of Islamic Walrasian equilibria is generically finite (and often unique) when israf bounds are tight.

Intuition: The israf bounds effectively compactify the choice set $X^I_i \cap [0, \bar{x}_i]$. This restriction can eliminate multiple equilibria that would exist in the unconstrained economy.

Why israf bounds help uniqueness:

  1. Reduced substitution: Agents cannot shift indefinitely between goods at the extremes, stabilizing demand
  2. Bounded choice sets: Compact feasible sets lead to well-behaved demand correspondences
  3. Price stability: The bounds prevent extreme price movements that characterize multiplicity

Trade-off: While uniqueness is enhanced, some allocations that would be equilibrium in the unconstrained economy are forbidden. This is intentionalIslamic constraints prioritize ethical compliance over unconstrained efficiency.

Comparative Statics

In the Islamic economy, changes in parameters have predictable effects:

Change Effect on Equilibrium
Increase in zakat rate Redistribution to the poor, but allocation remains Pareto optimal
Tighter israf bounds Reduced consumption at top, helps uniqueness
Change in halal classification Some goods excluded, demand shifts, prices adjust
PLS profit rate More investment in Islamic production

The Three Welfare Theorems in Islamic Economics

Theorem 6.1 (First Welfare Theorem - Islamic): Every Islamic Walrasian equilibrium is Pareto efficient relative to $\mathcal{F}^I$.

Theorem 6.2 (Second Welfare Theorem - Islamic with Zakat): Every Pareto efficient allocation in $\mathcal{F}^I$ can be supported as a competitive equilibrium via zakat redistribution.

Theorem 6.3 (Islamic Production Equilibrium): With PLS financing, the profit rate $\pi^*$ replaces the interest rate, and equilibrium exists with risk-sharing between investors and financiers.

Fundamental Insight

Islamic economics is not a set of ad-hoc restrictions on a conventional economy. Rather, it is a coherent alternative equilibrium theory where:

  1. Efficiency is redefined relative to the ethically constrained feasible set
  2. Zakat is endogenous to equilibrium, not an external tax
  3. PLS financing eliminates gharar and creates a fundamentally different market structure
  4. Uniqueness is often enhanced through Islamic constraints

The trade-off between unconstrained Pareto efficiency and ethical compliance is not a bugit is the feature. Islamic economics deliberately sacrifices some material allocative efficiency to achieve ethical and spiritual objectives.
