Why this project exists and what holds it together

Wamaal hayatud dunya illa mata'ul ghuroor

"The life of this world is nothing but the enjoyment of delusion."

Al-Quran 3:185

The Core Idea

This ayah, from the Quran (3:185), opens our homepage. It is also the intellectual anchor of this entire project.

It is not a call to reject the material world. The Quran repeatedly encourages trade, enterprise, and the pursuit of lawful provision. The earth is described as a trust, and working it is an act of worship. The point is not that the dunya is worthless. The point is that it is not the objective.

The Prophet (peace be upon him) was once asked what he feared most for his ummah. He did not name an external enemy. He described a disease he called wahn: love of the dunya and hatred of death. Not greed in the crude sense, but a quiet reorientation, where the material world shifts from being the arena in which you act to the thing for which you act. When that happens, the calculus changes. Priorities invert. And the consequences compound across every level of economic life.

This project exists because we believe that diagnosis is the most important idea in Islamic economics. Not the prohibition of interest. Not zakat. Not even the concept of justice, important as it is. All of those are downstream. The upstream question is: what are you optimizing for?

What This Project Is

For centuries, Islamic scholars developed sophisticated frameworks for understanding wealth, trade, and resource allocation, yet these contributions remain largely absent from mainstream economic discourse. Islamic Economics exists to bridge that gap. The platform brings together rigorous quantitative analysis and Islamic economic thought in one place, for scholars, students, policymakers, and anyone interested in understanding economics through an Islamic lens.

The site includes a textbook covering the principles of Islamic economics from introductory to intermediate level. A formal theory section that builds the mathematical framework from an alternative set of preference axioms to general equilibrium. A historical survey of Islamic economic institutions across fourteen centuries. Podcast series exploring sovereign debt, monetary systems, risk, trade, and more. Economic dashboards tracking real-time data across Muslim-majority economies. A research section with econometric studies and DSGE simulations. And a blog covering current developments in Islamic finance and the global Islamic economy.

This project is a work in progress and will be continuously updated and expanded. Feedback, corrections, and contributions are welcome.
