The Economic Cost of the Iran War: An Islamic Economics Perspective

By Editorial Desk March 11, 2026 10 min read Analysis

Key Takeaways

  • Operation Epic Fury (February 28, 2026) involved 900 US-Israeli strikes in 12 hours, killing over 1,000 and stranding hundreds of thousands of travelers.
  • Iran's retaliatory strikes hit UAE airports and Kuwait, disrupting 20% of global oil supply and creating the sharpest energy price spike since 2008.
  • Oil surged from ~$70 to over $120/barrel; the IEA released 400 million barrels in emergency reserves, the largest drawdown in history.
  • The crisis has devastated Muslim-majority economies most severely: Iran's rial collapsed to 1.4 million per dollar with 48.6% inflation, while Gulf production dropped 6.7 million barrels/day.
  • From an Islamic economics perspective, this represents both a failure of ummah (community) cooperation and a violation of Islamic jurisprudence prohibiting economic destruction during conflict.

The Cascade: From Military Escalation to Economic Crisis

On February 28, 2026, the regional conflict that had simmered for months between the United States, Israel, and Iran exploded into open warfare. In a coordinated campaign designated Operation Epic Fury, over 900 strikes rained down on Iranian military and governmental targets in the span of 12 hours. The strikes achieved their stated military objective with precision that reflected decades of intelligence gathering and drone technology refinement. But precision in military targeting does not prevent economic devastation.

The operation's most consequential moment came when strikes targeted Iran's supreme leadership. Ayatollah Khamenei, Iran's supreme leader and the figure who had defined Iranian policy for over three decades, was killed in the strikes. This decapitation of Iran's highest authority created an unprecedented power vacuum and triggered immediate retaliatory response. Within 48 hours, Iran launched ballistic and cruise missiles toward targets across the region. Some strikes reached the UAE, hitting Dubai and Abu Dhabi airports simultaneously. Others targeted Kuwaiti infrastructure.

The immediate casualties—over 1,000 deaths and hundreds of thousands of travelers stranded at airports from Dubai to Kuwait to Baghdad—dominated initial news coverage. But beneath these tragic human costs ran a deeper economic reality: in a matter of hours, the global energy supply chain had been disrupted, and the consequences would ripple through economies across the Muslim world and far beyond.

The Oil Crisis: Unprecedented Disruption of Global Supply

The Strait of Hormuz, a passage 21 miles wide at its narrowest point between Iran and Oman, handles approximately 20% of global oil supply—roughly 21 million barrels per day in normal times. This chokepoint has been the subject of international tension for decades, but conflict had never fully materialized. Now it had.

In the immediate aftermath of the strikes, shipping companies ceased transiting through the Strait. Port authorities in Dubai, Jebel Ali, and other Gulf facilities shut down operations. The UAE's Ports Authority announced temporary closure of multiple terminals. Kuwaiti oil operations slowed as workers evacuated from facilities and tanker traffic halted. Within 72 hours, approximately 6.7 million barrels per day of Gulf crude oil production had been taken offline—not through direct destruction of infrastructure, but through chaos, fear, and the operational paralysis that accompanies military escalation.

Oil markets, which had been trading around $70 per barrel in late February 2026, reacted with shock. Prices spiked to $110 within the first week. When initial assessments suggested the crisis would persist, prices climbed to $120 per barrel at their peak, representing a 71% increase in less than two weeks.

The International Energy Agency, recognizing the severity of the supply disruption, authorized the largest emergency petroleum reserve release in its history: 400 million barrels, coordinated among IEA members. This extraordinary measure, nearly double the previous record release during the 2011 Libya crisis, provided critical relief but also signaled to markets the severity of the perceived shortage.

Beyond crude oil, the disruption struck at a less-visible but equally important commodity: sulfur. The Persian Gulf region supplies approximately 45% of the world's elemental sulfur, a crucial input for fertilizer production and copper processing. When sulfur supply contracted, prices for both agricultural nutrients and copper spiked simultaneously, creating cascading effects across agriculture and industrial production globally.

The Immediate Global Fallout: Markets, Inflation, and Contagion

Stock markets worldwide reflected the shock. The Dow Jones Industrial Average declined 400+ points in the week following the initial strikes. Asian stock exchanges experienced broader declines as investors reassessed economic growth assumptions in a world of $120 oil. Bond markets moved in the opposite direction: government bonds rallied as investors sought safety, pushing US Treasury yields down sharply.

The inflation effects began immediately. Energy prices, already elevated from prior geopolitical tensions, spiked further. Transportation costs for goods rose, pushing up consumer prices at supermarkets from Southeast Asia to Europe to Latin America. The International Monetary Fund released preliminary estimates suggesting that if elevated oil prices persisted, inflation in oil-importing developing economies could rise by an additional 0.5% annually—not a staggering figure in isolation, but significant when applied to the hundreds of millions of consumers in Asia, Africa, and elsewhere already struggling with price pressures.

Central banks faced a policy dilemma. The natural response to inflation is to raise interest rates, tightening monetary conditions. But tightening during a negative supply shock (contraction of supply without contraction of demand) could trigger recession. The Bank of Japan, the European Central Bank, and the Federal Reserve all faced pressure to respond, but no response seemed appropriate. This was stagflation in motion: declining real output growth combined with rising price levels.

The Iran Economic Collapse: A Case Study in Economic Warfare

If the global economy faced disruption, Iran faced outright collapse. The nation's currency, the Iranian rial, had been under pressure for years. In 2000, the official exchange rate stood around 1,750 rials per US dollar. By 2024, following decades of sanctions, inflation, and capital flight, the rate had reached approximately 42,000 rials per dollar on official markets (though parallel markets had traded significantly higher). The February 2026 escalation accelerated what had been a long-term currency crisis into acute collapse. Within weeks, the rial traded at 1.4 million per dollar on parallel markets—a depreciation of over 3,200% in less than a generation.

Currency collapse inevitably produces hyperinflation. The official inflation rate reached 48.6% by March 2026, but this understated reality. Food prices, already elevated by prior inflation, surged by 72%. Staple items—bread, rice, oil—became unaffordable for millions of ordinary Iranians. The World Bank, in preliminary assessments, estimated that poverty rates had reached 22-50% depending on whether one used official or more realistic purchasing power metrics. The variation reflected uncertainty, but the direction was unmistakable: economic catastrophe.

Banks faced deposit flights as Iranian citizens, understanding the currency's instability, sought to move wealth into hard currencies. The central bank, with limited foreign exchange reserves, had no capacity to provide the dollars demanded. Some banks simply closed windows to prevent runs, a policy that existed nowhere in the developed world but had become routine in crisis economies. Unemployment spiked as businesses facing currency instability ceased operations or cut workforces.

This was no longer merely a financial crisis. It was a humanitarian crisis in motion. A country of 88 million people faced simultaneous currency collapse, inflation, unemployment, and capital flight. The International Committee of the Red Cross prepared humanitarian operations. The UN estimated that the conflict and its economic aftermath would require emergency food assistance for millions of Iranian civilians.

Economic Destruction: A Violation of Islamic Jurisprudence

From the perspective of Islamic economics and jurisprudence, this crisis presents a fundamental problem that transcends conventional geopolitical analysis. Islamic law contains extensive guidance on economic conduct during conflict, and the current situation violates multiple foundational principles.

Islamic jurisprudence, particularly as developed by classical scholars including al-Nawawi and Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyyah, distinguishes between legitimate military operations and illegitimate destruction of economic capacity. The principle is direct: in warfare, combatants may target military forces and government capabilities, but deliberate destruction of civilian economic infrastructure—markets, agricultural land, trade routes, productive capacity—is prohibited as a form of collective punishment.

The Prophet Muhammad explicitly prohibited the destruction of date palm orchards during the siege of Ta'if, not as a mercy to the enemy but as a principle: civilian productive capacity belongs to ordinary people. Destroying that capacity harms innocents whose only "crime" is their location. Classical Islamic scholars extended this principle: the permissibility of warfare against a state does not justify destruction of civilian livelihood sources.

The current escalation presents a more subtle violation. The direct military strikes on Iran were arguably legitimate acts of warfare directed at military and government targets. But the economic consequences—the paralysis of the Strait of Hormuz, the disruption of global oil supply, the resulting energy crisis affecting not merely Iran but all oil-importing nations—constitute a form of economic warfare that Islamic jurisprudence would classify as forbidden.

When the Strait of Hormuz is closed, it is not merely Iranian civilians who suffer. It is the consumers of oil-importing nations. It is small fishing communities in Yemen, Somalia, and other Gulf regions whose livelihoods depend on normal maritime trade. It is developing economies of Asia and Africa that face inflation and economic contraction. The disruption of global supply chains created collective economic punishment affecting billions of people who had no role in causing the conflict.

"There is no compulsion in religion. The right direction is clearly distinguished from error." (Quran 2:256) And "And do not cast yourselves into destruction with your own hands." (Quran 2:195)

These verses, while addressing different contexts, reflect a deeper Islamic principle: actions that destroy economic welfare and create conditions of desperation violate the Islamic framework. The current crisis has done precisely that. The sanctioning and blockading of Iran, even if economically justified by some Western policymakers as a means to influence Iranian government behavior, has instead produced mass civilian suffering—exactly the outcome Islamic jurisprudence intended to prohibit.

The Failure of Ummah Unity: Economic Fragmentation During Crisis

The Iran crisis has exposed a more profound Islamic economics challenge: the complete absence of collective economic response from the broader Muslim world. The ummah—the global community of Muslims—possesses theoretical principles of mutual support, economic cooperation, and collective responsibility. During this crisis, those principles have been entirely absent.

Consider the distribution of impact. Iran, a Muslim nation of 88 million people, faces economic collapse. The UAE, Bahrain, Saudi Arabia, and Kuwait—all Muslim-majority nations—have suffered direct economic damage from the regional conflict. Yet there has been no coordinated Muslim economic response: no solidarity fund, no trade bloc realignment to support affected economies, no joint commitment to rebuild regional economic infrastructure.

Instead, the response has been fragmented and conditional. Some Arab governments, aligned with the United States or Israel, have remained silent or ambiguously supportive of the escalation. Others have condemned the strikes on humanitarian grounds but taken no economic action. Turkey, a major regional power, has managed bilateral tensions but participated in no collective Muslim economic mobilization. Pakistan, Malaysia, Indonesia—economies representing over a billion Muslim consumers—have expressed concern but pursued no coordinated policy response.

This fragmentation reflects deeper structural problems in the Islamic economic system. The theoretical framework of Islamic economics emphasizes cooperation, zakat (obligatory charity), and mutual welfare. Yet in practice, Muslim-majority nations compete fiercely for market share, form alliances based on geopolitical interest rather than religious affinity, and provide minimal economic support to Muslim nations in crisis. The ummah, in economic terms, is a fiction.

A truly Islamic economic response to the Iran crisis would have involved immediate steps: coordinated reduction of oil supply competition (to prevent price spikes from benefiting non-Muslim producers at the expense of Muslim consumers), establishment of an emergency trade corridor bypassing disrupted routes, and establishment of a substantial relief fund supporting Iran's humanitarian crisis. None of these steps occurred because Muslim-majority nations lack the institutional capacity or political will to act collectively.

Sanctions as Siege Warfare: Islamic Jurisprudence on Economic Blockade

The economic devastation affecting Iran reflects not merely the immediate military conflict but years of intensifying economic sanctions imposed by Western nations. Islamic jurisprudence, developed in an era of medieval warfare, contains principles relevant to understanding sanctions.

Classical Islamic law prohibits hisar—siege—when the siege is deliberately designed to starve the civilian population. Al-Nawawi, the great Shafi'i jurist, argued that if a siege prevents access to food and medicine, and the ruler knows this will occur, the siege becomes prohibited even if the military target (the fortification or city) is legitimate. The principle: collective punishment through deprivation violates Islamic law.

Modern sanctions function precisely as historical sieges did. They restrict access to currency, financing, trade, and goods with the explicit aim of creating economic pressure on a population to force changes in their government's behavior. The mechanism may be different (international financial exclusion rather than military blockade), but the effect is identical: ordinary civilians face inflation, unemployment, and reduced access to imports.

The decades-long sanctions regime against Iran, intensified under various US administrations, created exactly the conditions Islamic jurisprudence prohibited: economic desperation. When people cannot access affordable food, medicine, and employment, they suffer. The Iranian government did not suffer proportionally; it maintained its military apparatus and security services while ordinary people bore the economic cost.

The February 2026 escalation, in this context, represented not a sharp break from prior policy but an acceleration of an existing reality. The combination of longstanding sanctions and military strikes has created simultaneous economic and military compression that Islamic jurisprudence would classify as prohibited siege warfare.

The Case for Economic Reconstruction and Alternative Financial Infrastructure

The Iran crisis, viewed through an Islamic economics lens, presents both a warning and an opportunity. The warning is clear: Muslim-majority nations remain economically fragmented, dependent on Western financial systems and dollar-based trade, vulnerable to disruption. The opportunity lies in the recognition that alternative models are possible and theoretically necessary.

Islamic economics emphasizes several principles directly applicable to the current crisis. First, reducing dependence on dollar-based international trade and Western financial infrastructure. The petrodollar system, where global oil is priced exclusively in US dollars, means that disruptions in US policy immediately create global economic shocks. A diversified system where significant oil trade occurred in other currencies, or even in commodity-backed currencies, would create resilience.

Second, Islamic economics emphasizes regional economic cooperation and trade blocs. The Organization of Islamic Cooperation (OIC), representing 57 Muslim-majority nations, possesses theoretical capacity to function as an economic union. Yet actual intra-OIC trade remains minimal—only about 5-8% of member states' total trade occurs within the bloc, compared to 60-70% for EU countries or ASEAN members. Deepening intra-Islamic trade, denominated in local or basket currencies rather than dollars, would reduce vulnerability to Western financial exclusion.

Third, Islamic finance principles—equity-based rather than debt-based, focusing on asset-backing rather than derivatives—could provide the foundation for more stable regional financial systems. A network of Islamic development banks, funded by member states' surpluses and coordinating investment in regional infrastructure, could reduce the necessity of Western financial integration.

None of this is technically impossible. Malaysia has developed a sophisticated Islamic financial system. The Islamic Development Bank possesses capital and institutional capacity. The Gulf Cooperation Council countries have accumulated sovereign wealth funds exceeding $3 trillion. The human capital, financial resources, and institutional knowledge exist to create alternative economic structures. What lacks is political will and the recognition that Western financial integration, while appearing normal and inevitable, actually leaves Muslim-majority economies vulnerable to precisely the kind of economic shock currently unfolding.

The Quranic Emphasis on Preservation of Life and Livelihood

Islamic economics, at its foundation, rests on the maqasid al-Shariah—the objectives of Islamic law. According to Islamic jurisprudence, these objectives are designed to preserve five essential things: life, religion, intellect, family/lineage, and property. In the context of economic systems, the emphasis is clear: economic policies should serve to preserve human life and livelihood, not destroy them.

The current crisis—with currency collapse, inflation, unemployment, and humanitarian suffering—represents the opposite of what Islamic economics aims to achieve. The Quran states: "And do not kill your children out of fear of poverty; We will provide for you and them" (17:31), reflecting a principle that economic desperation does not justify abandonment of social welfare. Yet in modern practice, economic sanctions and warfare are justified precisely on the grounds that they impose desperation that will force political change.

Islamic economics would argue for a different approach. Rather than economic destruction designed to force regime change, Islamic jurisprudence emphasizes economic development, trade, and mutual benefit as paths to stability. The Prophet Muhammad's emphasis on fair dealing, the prohibition on hoarding (ihtikar), the emphasis on market justice and preventing exploitation—these principles suggest an economic system oriented toward stability and mutual welfare rather than destabilization and collective punishment.

This is not naïve idealism. It reflects the pragmatic recognition that economic warfare creates humanitarian crises that generate extremism, refugee flows, and long-term instability far more destructive than the short-term disruption of military conflict. A system oriented toward preserving human welfare would prioritize rapid economic reconstruction, humanitarian aid, and normalization of trade over prolonged economic isolation.

Conclusion: Toward a Different Economic Architecture

The Iran crisis of February 2026 demonstrates the fragility of the current global economic system and the particular vulnerability of Muslim-majority economies within that system. The crisis represents not a unique shock but an acceleration of existing structural problems: over-reliance on dollar-denominated trade, minimal intra-Islamic trade and investment, lack of alternative financial infrastructure, and the vulnerability that fragmentation creates.

From the perspective of Islamic economics, the crisis additionally represents a violation of fundamental jurisprudential principles. Economic destruction as a tool of warfare, the use of sanctions as siege-warfare, and the failure of the ummah to maintain economic solidarity during crisis all contradict Islamic legal principles developed over centuries.

The path forward requires multiple elements. Immediately, it demands humanitarian response: emergency food, medical supplies, and reconstruction assistance for affected populations. Diplomatically, it requires recognition that economic destruction is counterproductive even from a realpolitik perspective. Long-term, it requires Muslim-majority economies to construct alternative economic infrastructure: deepening intra-Islamic trade, expanding Islamic finance capabilities, building regional supply chains less dependent on Western integration, and establishing mechanisms for economic cooperation during crises.

This is not anti-globalization or isolationist thinking. It is pragmatic recognition that true resilience requires reducing systemic dependencies. The wealthy nations of the world, including Western powers, maintain strategic reserves, diversified trade partners, and protected domestic industries specifically to avoid vulnerability to economic shocks. Muslim-majority economies have been encouraged to do the opposite: open their markets completely, depend on dollar-denominated trade, and rely on Western financial systems. The Iran crisis demonstrates the consequences of this asymmetry.

The Islamic economic framework offers principles for a different approach: risk-sharing rather than risk-shifting, asset-backing rather than derivatives, development-focused rather than extraction-focused finance, and community welfare as the explicit goal rather than an afterthought. These principles, applied at scale across Muslim-majority economies, would create both greater stability and stronger alignment with Islamic jurisprudential principles.

The crisis that unfolded on February 28, 2026, cannot be immediately reversed. But it can serve as a catalyst for fundamental rethinking of how Muslim-majority economies organize themselves, cooperate with each other, and insulate themselves from the economic warfare that seems increasingly normalized in international relations. That rethinking, from an Islamic economics perspective, is overdue.
