Beyond Borders: Why the Muslim World Needs a Unified Digital Payments System

By Guest Contributor February 15, 2026 7 min read Opinion

The Fragmentation Problem

When a Malaysian entrepreneur wants to purchase textiles from an Indonesian supplier, or when a Saudi charity seeks to transfer funds to a relief organization in Bangladesh, they encounter a labyrinth of intermediaries, currency conversions, and transaction fees that can consume up to 7% of the transfer value. This inefficiency stands in stark contrast to the seamless digital payment systems emerging within individual nations, and represents a missed opportunity for the 1.8 billion Muslims across 57 Organization of Islamic Cooperation (OIC) member states.

The World Bank estimates that remittances to OIC countries exceeded $400 billion in 2023, with intra-OIC trade valued at approximately $600 billion annually. Yet these massive flows remain shackled by fragmented payment infrastructures that rely heavily on Western-dominated networks like SWIFT, Visa, and Mastercard. The average cost of sending remittances within OIC countries stands at 6.8%, nearly double the United Nations Sustainable Development Goal target of 3%. This represents an annual drain of over $27 billion—resources that could otherwise fuel economic development, poverty alleviation, and entrepreneurship across the Muslim world.

The Islamic Imperative for Economic Cooperation

The case for a unified OIC digital payments infrastructure extends beyond mere economics into the realm of Islamic principle. The Quran explicitly encourages cooperation among believers: "And cooperate in righteousness and piety, but do not cooperate in sin and aggression" (5:2). Economic integration that facilitates halal commerce, reduces exploitation through excessive fees, and strengthens the collective capacity of Muslim nations represents a contemporary application of this divine directive.

The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) established the first Islamic state in Medina with a unified market system that eliminated arbitrary barriers to trade among diverse tribes. His prohibition of najash (artificial price inflation) and his emphasis on transparency in commercial transactions provide timeless principles for modern payment systems. A unified digital infrastructure could embed these Shariah-compliant principles at the architectural level, ensuring that every transaction across the Muslim world adheres to Islamic commercial ethics.

Moreover, the Islamic concept of the ummah—the global community of believers—calls for practical solidarity beyond rhetorical unity. When Turkish citizens face currency crises or when Pakistani businesses struggle with dollar shortages, a shared payment infrastructure could provide resilience through diversified settlement mechanisms and reduced dependence on external financial powers.

Technical Blueprint and Existing Models

The technological foundation for such a system already exists. Several models offer instructive precedents. The European Union's SEPA (Single Euro Payments Area) enables 36 countries to process euro transactions as seamlessly as domestic payments. China's Cross-Border Interbank Payment System (CIPS) has processed over $13 trillion in transactions since 2015, demonstrating how emerging economies can build alternative payment rails. India's Unified Payments Interface (UPI) has revolutionized domestic payments with over 10 billion monthly transactions, and is now expanding internationally.

An OIC digital payments infrastructure could leverage distributed ledger technology to create a decentralized yet interoperable network. The Islamic Development Bank (IsDB), with its $150 billion in assets and presence across member states, could serve as the coordinating institution. The system would require three core components: a standardized messaging protocol for payment instructions; a multilateral settlement mechanism supporting multiple currencies and potentially a digital Islamic settlement token; and shared compliance frameworks ensuring Shariah adherence and anti-money laundering standards.

Malaysia's success with DuitNow and the UAE's leadership in central bank digital currencies provide proof of concept within OIC nations. Indonesia's GPN (National Payment Gateway) and Turkey's TROY card system demonstrate the political will to build sovereign payment infrastructure. The challenge lies not in technical capability but in diplomatic coordination and commitment to shared infrastructure over purely national systems.

Addressing the Counterarguments

Critics reasonably point to the OIC's historical difficulty in implementing collective initiatives. The organization's mixed record on political coordination, they argue, suggests economic integration may prove equally elusive. Furthermore, wealthier Gulf states may question whether subsidizing payment infrastructure for poorer member nations serves their interests, while countries like Turkey and Malaysia might resist systems that compromise their technological sovereignty.

These concerns merit serious consideration but not capitulation. Unlike political disputes where zero-sum dynamics prevail, payment infrastructure creates positive-sum outcomes. Saudi Arabia and the UAE would benefit from reduced transaction costs with their largest trading partners and labor-sending countries. Turkey's technology sector could provide expertise and services to the network. Malaysia could position itself as a Shariah-compliance hub for the system.

The key lies in structuring governance to respect sovereignty while enabling interoperability. Rather than a monolithic system controlled by any single nation, the infrastructure should function as a network of networks—allowing national systems to retain autonomy while connecting through standardized protocols. This federated model, similar to email or mobile roaming, preserves national control while unlocking network effects.

A Roadmap Forward

Implementation should proceed incrementally, beginning with a pilot program among willing nations. The Gulf Cooperation Council countries, given their existing economic integration and technological sophistication, represent an ideal starting point. Success here could demonstrate viability and attract broader participation.

The IsDB should commission a comprehensive feasibility study by Q2 2025, followed by the establishment of a technical working group including central banks, fintech companies, and Shariah scholars. A minimal viable product connecting five to seven countries could launch by 2027, with full OIC integration targeted for 2030.

Funding could come from a combination of IsDB capital, member state contributions proportional to GDP, and private sector investment attracted by transaction fee revenue. The system should be designed as a public utility rather than a profit-maximizing enterprise, with fees set to cover costs and fund continuous improvement.

"O you who have believed, fulfill all contracts" (Quran 5:1)

The Muslim world stands at a crossroads. It can continue accepting fragmentation and dependency in its financial infrastructure, or it can fulfill the contract of cooperation that faith and shared interest demand. The technology exists. The economic case is compelling. What remains is the political will to transform the OIC from a talking shop into a builder of shared prosperity. A unified digital payments system would not solve all economic challenges facing Muslim-majority nations, but it would represent a tangible step toward the economic dignity and self-sufficiency that 1.8 billion Muslims deserve.
