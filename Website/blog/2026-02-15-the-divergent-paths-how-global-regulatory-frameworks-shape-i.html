The Divergent Paths: How Global Regulatory Frameworks Shape Islamic Banking's Future

By Policy Desk February 15, 2026 7 min read Policy & Governance

A Tale of Two Regulatory Philosophies

As Islamic finance assets surpass $3.5 trillion globally, a critical challenge has emerged that threatens to constrain the industry's next phase of growth: the stark regulatory divergence across jurisdictions. From Malaysia's comprehensive dual-banking framework to the United Kingdom's pragmatic accommodation approach, the absence of harmonized standards creates both opportunities and obstacles for institutions navigating the global Islamic economy.

The Islamic Financial Services Board (IFSB), established in 2002 and now counting 188 member institutions across 57 jurisdictions, has issued 23 standards covering everything from capital adequacy to governance. Yet implementation remains profoundly uneven. Malaysia's central bank, Bank Negara Malaysia, has embedded these standards into binding regulations through its Islamic Financial Services Act 2013, creating what many consider the gold standard of Islamic banking supervision. By contrast, some Gulf Cooperation Council nations maintain lighter-touch regimes that prioritize market growth over regulatory rigor, while Western jurisdictions like the UK and Luxembourg have opted for a principles-based approach that accommodates Islamic finance within existing frameworks rather than creating parallel systems.

The Malaysian Model: Comprehensive Integration

Malaysia's regulatory architecture offers the most developed template for Islamic banking supervision. The country's dual-banking system, which allows conventional and Islamic banks to operate side-by-side under distinct but equally robust regulatory frameworks, serves approximately 140 Islamic banking institutions holding assets exceeding $240 billion. Bank Negara Malaysia's Shariah Governance Framework, updated in 2019, mandates that Islamic financial institutions maintain Shariah committees with at least five members, including at least two with relevant expertise in Islamic commercial law.

The Malaysian approach extends beyond mere compliance monitoring. The central bank has established the International Shariah Research Academy for Islamic Finance (ISRA) to develop scholarly foundations for regulatory decisions, while the Shariah Advisory Council of Bank Negara Malaysia serves as the ultimate authority on Shariah matters in Islamic finance. This institutional depth has enabled Malaysia to develop sophisticated Islamic capital markets, including the world's largest sukuk market, which issued instruments worth $51 billion in 2023 alone.

Yet Malaysia's comprehensive approach comes with costs. Compliance requirements are substantial, with Islamic banks facing dual reporting obligations and higher operational expenses than their counterparts in less regulated markets. Small and medium-sized Islamic financial institutions particularly struggle with the regulatory burden, leading to industry consolidation that may ultimately reduce competition.

The GCC's Growth-First Approach

The Gulf states have adopted markedly different strategies, reflecting their position as the historical heartland of Islamic finance. The UAE's dual regulatory system, with the Dubai Financial Services Authority (DFSA) overseeing the Dubai International Financial Centre and the Central Bank of the UAE regulating onshore institutions, exemplifies a more flexible approach. The DFSA's Islamic Finance Rulebook, while comprehensive, allows greater latitude in product structuring and governance arrangements than Malaysia's framework.

Saudi Arabia's Capital Market Authority has taken an increasingly assertive stance since the kingdom's Vision 2030 economic transformation program began. The introduction of mandatory AAOIFI (Accounting and Auditing Organization for Islamic Financial Institutions) standards in 2017 represented a significant shift toward standardization. However, implementation has been gradual, with the Saudi Arabian Monetary Authority (now the Saudi Central Bank) balancing regulatory rigor against the imperative to rapidly expand Islamic banking services to support economic diversification. The kingdom's Islamic banking assets reached $685 billion in 2023, representing 82% of total banking assets.

This growth-oriented approach has yielded impressive market expansion but raised questions about systemic risk management. The lack of standardized stress-testing frameworks specific to Islamic banks' unique risk profiles, particularly around profit-sharing investment accounts and commodity murabaha transactions, has drawn criticism from the IFSB and international observers.

Western Accommodation and Its Limits

Western jurisdictions have generally opted for accommodation rather than specialized regulation. The United Kingdom, home to approximately $5 billion in Islamic banking assets, treats Islamic banks as conventional institutions subject to Prudential Regulation Authority oversight, while allowing flexibility in product structuring to achieve Shariah compliance. The Bank of England's 2014 introduction of a Shariah-compliant liquidity facility represented a significant milestone, yet the UK has resisted creating separate regulatory categories for Islamic finance.

This approach has advantages in reducing regulatory fragmentation and signaling that Islamic finance is mainstream rather than niche. However, it creates challenges around issues like deposit insurance (which conflicts with profit-and-loss sharing principles) and taxation of Islamic financing structures that may involve multiple transactions to achieve economic outcomes equivalent to conventional interest-based lending. Luxembourg and France have made targeted tax adjustments to level the playing field, but comprehensive solutions remain elusive.

The Path Toward Harmonization

The case for regulatory harmonization grows stronger as Islamic finance becomes increasingly cross-border. The IFSB's core principles for Islamic finance regulation, while not binding, provide a foundation. Yet meaningful convergence requires political will that has thus far proven elusive. The Organization of Islamic Cooperation's member states have widely varying economic systems, legal traditions, and interpretations of Shariah principles, making comprehensive standardization perhaps unrealistic.

A more pragmatic path may lie in mutual recognition agreements between jurisdictions with robust Islamic banking frameworks. Malaysia and Indonesia's 2016 memorandum of understanding on Islamic finance supervision offers a model, enabling easier cross-border operations for institutions meeting either jurisdiction's standards. The GCC's ongoing work toward a unified Islamic banking framework, though progressing slowly, could create a significant regulatory bloc.

"O you who have believed, fulfill [all] contracts." (Quran 5:1)

This Quranic principle of contractual sanctity underlies Islamic finance, yet its regulatory implementation varies dramatically across jurisdictions. As the industry matures, the tension between local flexibility and global standardization will likely intensify. The jurisdictions that balance these imperatives most effectively, maintaining Shariah authenticity while enabling cross-border scalability, will likely emerge as the Islamic finance centers of tomorrow. For now, regulatory divergence remains both the industry's greatest challenge and, paradoxically, a source of competitive differentiation that drives innovation across markets.
