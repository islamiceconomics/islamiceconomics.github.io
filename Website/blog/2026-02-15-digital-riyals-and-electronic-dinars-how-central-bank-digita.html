Digital Riyals and Electronic Dinars: How Central Bank Digital Currencies Are Reshaping Islamic Finance

By Policy Desk February 15, 2026 7 min read Policy & Governance

The Digital Currency Revolution Reaches the Muslim World

As central banks worldwide race to develop digital currencies, Islamic financial institutions face a unique challenge: ensuring these new monetary instruments comply with Shari'ah principles while maintaining their competitive edge in an increasingly digitized global economy. The past eighteen months have witnessed unprecedented momentum, with the Central Bank of the UAE completing its CBDC strategy roadmap in late 2023, Saudi Arabia's Project Aber advancing to pilot phases, and Malaysia's Bank Negara announcing plans for a wholesale CBDC by 2026.

The stakes are considerable. The Islamic finance industry, valued at approximately $3.9 trillion globally, must now grapple with fundamental questions about the nature of digital money, its compatibility with Islamic economic principles, and its potential to either enhance or undermine the core values of equitable wealth distribution that underpin Islamic finance. Unlike conventional banking systems, where CBDCs primarily represent technological upgrades, Islamic finance must ensure these digital instruments preserve the asset-backing requirements, prohibition of riba (interest), and real economic activity linkage that distinguish Islamic monetary theory.

Shari'ah Compliance in the Digital Age

The International Islamic Fiqh Academy, in its recent deliberations, has begun addressing the permissibility of CBDCs under Islamic law. The central question revolves around whether digital currencies can fulfill the requirements of 'mal' (wealth) and 'thaman' (price) as defined in classical Islamic jurisprudence. According to the majority scholarly opinion emerging from Dubai and Kuala Lumpur, CBDCs can be considered legitimate money provided they meet three conditions: government backing that ensures acceptance, stability of value, and freedom from riba-based mechanisms in their issuance and distribution.

The Accounting and Auditing Organization for Islamic Financial Institutions (AAOIFI) has established a specialized committee to develop standards for digital currency transactions. Their preliminary findings suggest that CBDCs, unlike cryptocurrencies such as Bitcoin, possess characteristics more aligned with Shari'ah requirements due to their centralized nature and state backing. Dr. Mohammed Al-Jasser, former governor of the Saudi Arabian Monetary Authority, noted in a recent address that "CBDCs represent an opportunity to embed Islamic principles directly into monetary architecture, potentially creating the most Shari'ah-compliant form of money in modern history."

However, scholars remain vigilant about implementation details. The prohibition of gharar (excessive uncertainty) requires that CBDC systems maintain transparency in their operation. The principle of 'adalah (justice) demands that digital currency access doesn't create new forms of financial exclusion. As the Quran states:

"O you who believe, do not consume one another's wealth unjustly, but only through trade mutually agreed upon." (Quran 4:29)
This verse underscores the requirement that any monetary system, digital or otherwise, must facilitate genuine economic exchange rather than speculative accumulation.

Operational Implications for Islamic Banks

Islamic financial institutions are preparing for significant operational changes as CBDCs move from concept to reality. The Islamic Development Bank has allocated $150 million toward digital infrastructure development across member states, recognizing that CBDC adoption will require substantial technological upgrades. For Islamic banks, the transition presents both opportunities and challenges in their core product offerings.

Murabaha (cost-plus financing) transactions, which constitute approximately 60% of Islamic banking products globally, could see enhanced efficiency through CBDC settlement. Smart contracts embedded in digital currencies could automatically verify asset transfer and payment obligations, reducing documentation requirements and settlement times from days to seconds. Kuwait Finance House has already piloted CBDC-based murabaha transactions in partnership with the Central Bank of Kuwait, reporting a 78% reduction in processing time and enhanced transparency in profit-sharing calculations.

Sukuk markets stand to benefit substantially from CBDC integration. The tokenization of Islamic bonds through central bank digital currencies could democratize access to these instruments, currently dominated by institutional investors. Malaysia's Securities Commission has proposed a framework where CBDC-denominated sukuk could be issued in fractional amounts as small as 100 ringgit ($23), compared to current minimum investments of 250,000 ringgit. This aligns with the Islamic principle of broad-based wealth distribution and could channel retail savings more effectively into productive economic activities.

Yet concerns persist about disintermediation. If customers can hold central bank money directly through digital wallets, the deposit base of Islamic banks could erode, affecting their ability to provide financing. The Islamic Financial Services Board has recommended a two-tier CBDC model where Islamic banks continue to play intermediary roles, ensuring that the principle of risk-sharing—fundamental to Islamic finance—remains embedded in the monetary system rather than being bypassed through direct central bank-to-consumer relationships.

Cross-Border Implications and Regional Integration

Perhaps the most transformative potential of CBDCs for Islamic finance lies in cross-border transactions. The Gulf Cooperation Council's Project Aber, a joint initiative between Saudi Arabia and the UAE, has successfully tested wholesale CBDC transfers, reducing cross-border settlement times from two days to mere seconds while cutting costs by approximately 40%. For a region where intra-GCC trade exceeds $150 billion annually, such efficiency gains are substantial.

The Organization of Islamic Cooperation has proposed an ambitious vision: a network of interoperable Islamic CBDCs that could facilitate trade among its 57 member states without reliance on dollar-denominated correspondent banking. This resonates with historical Islamic economic practices, where the gold dinar and silver dirham facilitated trade across vast territories from Spain to Indonesia. Turkey's central bank has been particularly vocal about this possibility, with Governor Şahap Kavcıoğlu stating that "digital currencies offer Muslim-majority nations an opportunity to rebuild monetary sovereignty while remaining integrated with global financial systems."

Indonesia and Malaysia have begun exploratory talks on linking their respective CBDC initiatives, potentially creating a digital currency corridor for the world's two largest Muslim-majority economies. With combined trade of $18 billion annually, a shared digital settlement infrastructure could significantly reduce transaction costs for small and medium enterprises, which form the backbone of both economies and are often underserved by conventional correspondent banking.

The Path Forward: Balancing Innovation and Principle

As central bank digital currencies transition from experimentation to implementation, Islamic finance institutions face a critical juncture. The technology offers unprecedented opportunities to embed Islamic principles directly into monetary infrastructure—ensuring transparency, reducing speculation, and facilitating real economic activity. The challenge lies in ensuring that the rush to digitize doesn't compromise the ethical foundations that distinguish Islamic finance from its conventional counterpart.

The next two years will be decisive. With the UAE planning to launch its digital dirham pilot in 2025 and Saudi Arabia targeting full CBDC implementation by 2027, Islamic financial institutions must actively participate in design decisions rather than merely adapting to predetermined structures. The Bahrain-based International Islamic Financial Market has called for the establishment of a unified Shari'ah advisory board specifically for CBDCs, ensuring consistent standards across jurisdictions.

The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said: "The truthful and trustworthy merchant will be in the company of the Prophets, the truthful, and the martyrs." This hadith reminds us that Islamic finance's ultimate purpose transcends mere compliance—it seeks to create economic systems that serve human welfare and social justice. Central bank digital currencies, properly designed and implemented, could advance this vision, creating monetary systems that are not only technologically sophisticated but also ethically grounded. The question is whether policymakers and financial institutions will seize this opportunity or allow it to pass unrealized.
