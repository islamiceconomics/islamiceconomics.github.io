Why Comparative Religious Economics?

The Abrahamic traditions share far more economic common ground than most people realize. All three originally prohibited lending at interest. All three mandate care for the poor as a matter of justice, not charity alone. All three place limits on the accumulation and use of property. And all three ground their economic teachings in a theology of divine ownership and human stewardship.

Yet today these traditions occupy vastly different positions. Christianity largely accommodated interest-based finance through centuries of Scholastic reinterpretation. Judaism developed sophisticated legal instruments to navigate between prohibition and commercial reality. Islam maintained the formal prohibition while building an alternative financial architecture. Understanding how traditions that began in the same place arrived at such different destinations is not merely a historical exercise. It reveals the mechanisms by which economic theology evolves, adapts, and sometimes loses its original force.

This section applies the analytical structure of economics itself to the comparison: beginning with the epistemological question of where economic knowledge comes from, moving through the microeconomic foundations of property, exchange, and interest, then to the macroeconomic frameworks of money, distribution, and the state, and concluding with the historical question of divergence and the possibility of convergence. Each chapter examines the three traditions side by side, drawing on primary sources from the Quran, Bible, Torah, Talmud, and the major scholarly traditions within each faith.

Chapter Outline

Part I: Foundations

1. Epistemology & Sources of Economic Knowledge

Where does economic knowledge come from? Islam derives it from revelation (Quran, Sunnah) interpreted through ijma and qiyas. Christianity blends Scripture with the natural law tradition formalized by Aquinas. Judaism grounds it in Torah and the evolving rabbinic discourse of the Talmud and responsa literature. These epistemological differences determine how each tradition adapts to new economic realities and whether it can.

2. Axioms of Economic Behaviour

What does each tradition assume about human nature and economic motivation? The homo economicus of neoclassical theory, the homo islamicus of Islamic economics, and the varying Christian anthropologies from Augustinian pessimism to Calvinist vocation. How each faith understands self-interest, rationality, the role of the soul in economic decision-making, and whether humans are fundamentally trustees or owners of what they possess.

Part II: Microeconomics

3. Property Rights & Ownership

The analytical foundation. What can be owned, what are the limits on ownership, and what is the theory of original acquisition? Divine ownership and human trusteeship in Islam, the natural rights tradition in Christianity, and the concept of bounded ownership in Jewish law. How each tradition handles commons, inheritance, and the tension between individual rights and communal welfare.

4. Exchange & Contract Theory

What makes a transaction valid and just? Conditions for contract formation, the treatment of uncertainty (gharar), information asymmetry, and just price theory across the three traditions. Aquinas and the Scholastics, Islamic muamalat jurisprudence, and Talmudic commercial law: three sophisticated legal frameworks addressing the same fundamental problem of fair exchange.

5. The Prohibition of Interest

The central comparative question. All three traditions originally prohibited usury, but only Islam maintained a broad prohibition into the modern era. This chapter traces the theological reasoning, the historical process of reinterpretation in Christianity, the development of the heter iska in Judaism, and the construction of Islamic alternatives such as mudarabah and musharakah. Why did these traditions diverge on what was once shared ground?

6. Labour, Firms & Production

The dignity of work, the right to fair wages, and the obligations of employers and employees. How each tradition conceptualizes the purpose of the firm, the ethics of profit, and the relationship between capital and labour. From the Prophetic hadith on paying the worker before his sweat dries, to Catholic social teaching on the living wage, to Talmudic employment law.

Part III: Macroeconomics

7. Money & Monetary Theory

What is money, and what can legitimately be done with it? Commodity money versus fiat money, the ethics of money creation, and debasement. Ibn Taymiyyah's monetary thought alongside Nicole Oresme's treatise on coinage and the Talmudic laws of currency. The relationship between monetary theory and the prohibition of interest, and why a tradition's view of money determines its entire financial architecture.

8. Distribution & Social Obligation

How should wealth be redistributed, and is it a matter of obligation or generosity? Zakat as a mandatory pillar of Islam with precise rates and categories. Christian tithing as it evolved from obligation to voluntarism. Tzedakah in Judaism as justice rather than charity. Waqf, monastic endowments, and communal funds as institutional mechanisms. The spectrum from state-administered redistribution to community-based welfare.

9. The Role of the State

What is the proper relationship between political authority and economic life? The Islamic institution of hisbah (market supervision), canon law's influence on medieval European commerce, and the Talmudic concept of dina d'malkhuta dina (the law of the land is the law). Taxation, price controls, public goods, and the boundaries between religious and secular economic governance.

Part IV: Synthesis

10. Historical Divergence & Modern Convergence

The empirical and analytical conclusion. When and why did three traditions that shared so much economic common ground end up in such different places? The role of the Reformation, the rise of commercial capitalism, colonialism, and the modern Islamic finance revival. Whether the growing critique of interest-based finance in secular economics, combined with the renewed interest in ethical and faith-based finance, represents a convergence point. What each tradition has preserved, what it has lost, and what it might recover from the others.
